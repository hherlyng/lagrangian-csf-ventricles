\begin{abstract}
    The dynamic flow of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) in brain ventricles 
    exhibits flow features on several scales, both spatially and temporally.
    Analyzing this complex time-dependent flow and the accompanying transport
    through instantaneous (Eulerian) flow variables is normal, but instantaneous
    variables can deceive understanding of flow, especially time dependence.
    Here, we analyze brain ventricular CSF flow both in a Eulerian
    sense and from the Lagrangian perspective -- a time-integrated view of the flow.
    With geometries generated from medical imaging data, we model CSF flow
    in adult human and embroynic zebrafish brain ventricles. 
    In the human brain we model flow governed by cardiac pulsations,
    CSFsecretion and motile cilia. 
    The flow driven by cardiac pulsations is derived from a damped linear elastic model
    of brain ventricle deformations, as a result of applying displacement boundary
    conditions, with the boundary data derived from experimental data.
    In the zebrafish brain we consider flow driven solely by motile cilia.
    The solid and flow models are implemented and solved with finite element methods.
    We use the resulting velocity fields to compute finite-time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE)
    fields and use these fields to characterize Lagrangian coherent structures,
    which can be approximated by ridges in the FTLE fields.
    These coherent structures demonstrate prominent flow features in the brain ventricles that resonate
    well with findings in experimental research. In the human brain ventricles, we also 
    investigate the role of inertia by comparing flow models governed by 
    the Navier-Stokes and the Stokes equations. Comparisons show that
    solving the Stokes equations is adequate to compute integrated flow
    variables like stroke volumes, but that the Stokes approximation fails 
    to resolve intricate features of flow and advective transport which
    are present in the solution to the Navier-Stokes equations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% I1: What is the problem - from big picture to our precise topic - why is it interesting?
Brains are constantly in motion. These motions are tightly coupled
to the cardiac and respiratory cycles, bodily movement and
neural activity. This brain motion is a key contributor to
cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow~\cite{Enzmann1992BrainImaging,
baledent2001cerebrospinal, sweetman2011cerebrospinal}.
CSF is produced by choroid plexus in the brain ventricles,
from where the fluid flows into the spinal cord or
to the subarachnoid space~\cite{rasmussen2018glymphatic}.
The flow of CSF is an important contributor to brain homeostasis, delivery of
nutrients, and CSF bathes the cortex such that the fluid
serves as a cushion for the brain~\cite{fame2020emergence,
kelley2023cerebrospinal, linninger2016cerebrospinal}.
Meanwhile, several pathologies afflicting the brain
are related to disruption of CSF flow,
such as hydrocephalus, where ventricular enlargement
results from blockage of CSF flow~\cite{linninger2016cerebrospinal}.
Although CSF is fundamental to brain functioning,
the underlying mechanisms of CSF flow are still not
completely understood~\cite{kelley2023cerebrospinal}.

% I2: What do we know about the problem and the topic?
The nature of CSF flow has been studied extensively
in recent decades, both experimentally and computationally.
Among the physiological processes that affect CSF flow are:
blood vessel and respiration dynamics,
production and absorption of CSF,
beating of motile cilia, neural activity, and posture changes.
\cite{kelley2023cerebrospinal, sweetman2011cerebrospinal,
Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment, Faubel2016CiliaVentricles,
Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius, Czosnyka2004CerebrospinalDynamics,
Vinje2019RespiratoryMeasurements, Liu2024UsingBreathing,
baledent2001cerebrospinal}.
With these physical processes occuring
over various time and length scales, CSF flow
also consists of motions on different
scales~\cite{kelley2023cerebrospinal}.
In the brain ventricles, large-scale, pulsatile bulk motion
is induced by pulsations related to
the cardiac and respiratory cycles, both cycles
dynamically changing the blood volume in the brain
which deforms surrounding tissue and results in CSF flow~\cite{enzmann1991normal,
wagshul2011pulsating, Liu2024UsingBreathing, baledent2004relationship,
greitz1992pulsatile, Vinje2019RespiratoryMeasurements,
kelley2023cerebrospinal, greitz1993pulsatile}.
Back-and-forth motion of CSF with vortical flow features
has been visualized in human brains~\cite{stadlbauer2010insight},
and the pulsatility is clearly present in measurements of flow rates
\cite{baledent2004relationship, Liu2024UsingBreathing, Vinje2019RespiratoryMeasurements}.
At smaller scales, visualizations of ventricular
CSF flow trajectories in animals have demonstrated
intricate features such as stagnation and separation points,
and vortex-structured flows created by motile cilia
\cite{gilpin2017flowtrace, Faubel2016CiliaVentricles,
Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment}.
Cilia-driven CSF flow has also 
to a lesser extent been studied in humans~\cite{worthington1963ependymal,
Yoshida2022EffectVentricles, Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles},
demonstrating local effects of cilia on CSF flow patterns.
Some flow patterns such as separatrices and vortices are examples of material structures
in fluid flows, often referred to as Lagrangian coherent
structures (LCS), that are responsible for stretching and 
folding in the mixing of passive tracers~\cite{haller2015lagrangian,
haller2000lagrangian}. LCS can be characterized by computing
finite-time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE) fields through integration of fluid
parcel trajectories; ridges of these FTLE fields are good approximations of
LCS~\cite{shadden2005definition}. Characterizing LCS thus provides
a time-integrated view of fluid transport
\cite{shadden2005definition, haller2015lagrangian, haller2000lagrangian}.
The explanatory power of LCS has previously been demonstrated
in a host of applications, including blood flow
\cite{shadden2008characterization, shadden2010computational},
atmospheric flow~\cite{Gunther2021LagrangianWinds},
flow in airways~\cite{lukens2010using}
and oceanic flows~\cite{beron2015dissipative}.

% I3: What do we not know about the problem and what do we want to
% know? What are the precise research questions we want to address?
% Refer to specific studies and what specifically we do not know.
Despite the predictive power of studying flow from a
Lagrangian perspective--where fluid parcel trajectories are tracked over time--only
a limited amount of previous CSF flow studies
have considered this perspective~\cite{Yoshida2022EffectVentricles}.
Most studies are rather based on flow analysis with an
Eulerian perspective~\cite{Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius,
Causemann2022HumanFramework, howden2008three, Sweetman2011ThreeDimensional,
Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles},
in which flow variables such as velocity and volumetric flux
are measured at a fixed point in space and time.
Instantaneous flow variables may however deceive
interpretation of time-dependence in the flow~\cite{shadden2005definition},
especially in flows that contain motion at multiple scales,
such as ventricular CSF flow. Therefore,
further knowledge of flow and transport in brain ventricles,
and the precise contributions of specific
flow mechanisms such as cardiac pulsations, CSF production,
and beating of motile cilia to mixing and transport of CSF,
could be gained from Lagrangian analysis.
For example, do LCS exist in brain ventricular CSF flow,
and if so, how are the LCS organized?
How are would the LCS be affected by flow mechanisms
such as cardiac pulsations, motile cilia and CSF production?
Does inertia play a significant role in establishing the
LCS and thus the advective transport within the brain ventricles?

% I4: How do we address the problem and what do we find out?
In this work, we characterize LCS in brain ventricular CSF flow
using a computational model. We model intraventricular CSF flow
in brain ventricles of adult human and embryonic zebrafish using geometries
generated with medical imaging data. Using finite element methods,
we simulate brain deformation and CSF flow. The CSF velocity fields are
used to calculate particle trajectories.
These trajectories are used to compute finite-time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE) fields, which
we use to characterize LCS. In the human brain, we model
flow driven by cardiac pulsations, choroid plexus secretion and motile cilia,
and study the impact of each mechanism on flow patterns and advective transport.
For the zebrafish brain, we model flow driven by motile cilia, and demonstrate
that FTLE fields can be used to characterize LCS that reflect flow trajectories
previously observed in experiments. Altogether, our work demonstrates the usefulness of
Lagrangian analysis in furthering our understanding of CSF flow.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Image-based computational ventricular geometries}

we consider two brain ventricular geometries based on medical imaging data
\Cref{fig:model_sketch}. One is the ventricular geometry
of an embryonic zebrafish at two days post-fertilization (2 dpf). The surface geometry was in previous work
generated with confocal microscopy~\cite{Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment}. At 2 dpf, the zebrafish
brain ventricular system consists of three cavities: the anterior, middle and posterior
ventricles. The three compartments are connected by interventricular ducts. A volumetric
mesh constituting 132 134 tetrahedral cells, with a maximal (minimal) edge
length of 14.9 \textmu m (4.7 \textmu m), was generated with

system are denoted in~\Cref{fig:model_sketch}. The standard version of the ventricles mesh
consists of 408 128 tetrahedral cells with maximal and minimal edge lengths

\subsection{The governing equations of brain ventricular cerebrospinal fluid flow}
We model CSF flow as the flow of an incompressible,
Newtonian fluid of density $\rho=1000 \ \mathrm{kg/m^3}$ and dynamic viscosity
$\mu=0.7 \ \mathrm{mPa\cdot s}$~\cite{Bloomfield1998EffectsFluid}
in a domain $\DD\subset\mathbb{R}^d$
with boundary $\partial\DD=\Scal$.
Neglecting the graviational acceleration, no body forces are acting on the fluid.
The flow is governed by the Navier-Stokes equations:
\begin{alignat}{1}
    \rho\left(\pdifft{\uu}
    + (\uu\cdot\nabla)\uu\right) &= \nabla\cdot\TT,
    \label{eq:navier_stokes_mom_eq} \\
    \nabla\cdot\uu &= 0,
    \label{eq:continuity_eq}
\end{alignat}
with the stress tensor $\TT$ defined as
\begin{equation*}
    \TT = 2\mu\beps(\uu) - p\mathbb{I},
\end{equation*}
where $\uu(\xx, t)$ and $p(\xx, t)$
are the fluid velocity and pressure,
$\mathbb{I}$ is the identity tensor in dimension $d$ and
$\beps(\uu)=\frac{1}{2}\left(\nabla\uu + (\nabla\uu)^T\right)$ is the
strain-rate tensor.

The brain ventricles deform over time and thus 
the domain $\DD$ is deforming and varies over time.
Therefore, we solve the Navier-Stokes equations with an
Arbitrary Lagrangian Eulerian (ALE) approach~\cite{Duarte2004ALE}.
The governing equations are solved in the reference configuration $\chi$,
using the mapping $\psi : \xx\mapsto\chi$ from the current deformed
configuration at time $t$ to the reference configuration.
In this setup, the governing equations are
\begin{alignat}{1}
    \rho\left(\pdifft{\uu} + ((\uu - \cc)\cdot\hat{\nabla})\uu\right)
    &= \hat{\nabla}\cdot\TT \label{eq:ALE_momentum_eq} \\
    \hat{\nabla}\cdot\uu &= 0 \label{eq:ALE_continuity_eq}
\end{alignat}
where $\cc$ is the displacement velocity and
$\hat{\nabla}$ is the transformed differential operator accounting
for the mapping $\psi$.
In \Cref{eq:ALE_momentum_eq} and \Cref{eq:ALE_continuity_eq},
all variables are evaluated in the reference configuration.
Like for the differential operator $\nabla$, integrals of the weak formulation
must be transformed appropriately to account for the
domain motion~\cite{Scovazzi2007LectureNotes}.

For fluid flows where viscous forces dominate inertial 
forces, the Stokes equations
\begin{equation}
    - \nabla\cdot\TT = \bm{0}
    \label{eq:stokes_eq}
\end{equation}
are often used as an approximation to the momentum equation (\Cref{eq:navier_stokes_mom_eq}).
Viscous forces dominate in CSF flow in smaller brains
such as embryonic zebrafish brain ventricles
\cite{Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment, Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion},
and therefore we use \Cref{eq:stokes_eq} to model CSF flow
in zebrafish. In the human brain, flow measurements and simulations
indicate that CSF flow is in a laminar regime
with non-negligible transient effects, but negligible
inertial effects~\cite{Gholampour2018FSI, howden2008three}.
In such a flow regime the time-dependent Stokes equations
\begin{equation}
    \rho\pdifft{\uu} - \nabla\cdot\TT = \bm{0}
    \label{eq:time_dependent_stokes_eq}
\end{equation}
may serve as a fair approximation.
In~\Cref{eq:time_dependent_stokes_eq}, the convective acceleration term
$(\uu\cdot\nabla)\uu$ is neglected, while the local acceleration
$\rho\partial\uu/\partial t$ is retained to account for transient effects.
Several previous computational studies of CSF flow are
based on \Cref{eq:time_dependent_stokes_eq}
\cite{Causemann2022HumanFramework, kelley2023cerebrospinal}.
We will compute flow in human brain ventricles with both the Navier-Stokes
equations (\Cref{eq:navier_stokes_mom_eq}) and the time-dependent
Stokes equations (\Cref{eq:time_dependent_stokes_eq}) to assess
the role of inertia.

\subsection{Flow mechanisms and boundary conditions}
CSF flow in adult human brain ventricles differs from CSF flow in embryonic
zebrafish brain ventricles. Therefore, we consider different flow mechanisms
and boundary conditions for the two geometries.
In the human brain, we consider three flow mechanisms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Deformation of the brain ventricles related to cardiac pulsatility,
    modeled by imposing a displacement velocity
    on the ventricular walls (see~\Cref{subsubsec:deformation})
    \item Secretion of CSF from choroid plexus tissue,
    modeled by imposing a velocity normal to the wall
    which reflects a total influx of CSF (see~\Cref{subsubsec:secretion})
    \item Ciliary beating, modeled by a tangential traction
    acting on the fluid at the boundary (see~\Cref{subsubsec:cilia})
\end{itemize}
The main driver of intraventricular CSF flow in humans is
pulsatility related to the cardiac and respiratory cycles~\cite{wagshul2011pulsating,
Vinje2019RespiratoryMeasurements, sweetman2011cerebrospinal, baledent2001cerebrospinal}. 
During systole, the blood vessels in the brain expand and followingly also
the parenchyma, putting pressure on the ventricular system. The inward displacement
of the brain ventricles leads to antegrade (from cephalic to caudal) CSF flow.
When the blood vessels relax and contract during diastole, the flow reverses and
CSF flows back into the system~\cite{sweetman2011cerebrospinal, greitz1992pulsatile}.
The respiratory cycle also influences CSF flow pressure 
gradients, but at different frequencies than the cardiac cycle~\cite{Vinje2019RespiratoryMeasurements}.
In this study, we model deformations of the ventricles with a cardiac cycle frequency.

In addition to pulsatility, we model CSF production and ciliary beating
that both contribute to CSF flow in the human brain ventricles.
CSF is mainly produced by the choroid plexus tissue in the lateral,
third and fourth ventricles (\Cref{fig:model_sketch}). Motile cilia
are abundant in the human brain~\cite{doetsch1997cellular,
scott1973scanning, coletti2018characterization, worthington1963ependymal},
but are not found in the caudal third of the third ventricle~\cite{scott1972comparative,
bruni1972scanning, sanchez2024exploring} nor on choroid plexus~\cite{scott1972comparative,
ho2023choroid, narita2015cilia}. Motile cilia are hair-like structures that line
the ependyma of brain ventricular walls, and the beating motion of cilia propel
CSF~\cite{Ringers2020Role, Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment}.

A similar description of cardiac pulsatility as given above does not exist 
for embryonic zebrafish brain ventricles~\cite{Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion}.
Moreover, choroid plexus secretion begins at a later developmental stage than 2 dpf % Cite this
in zebrafish. Therefore, we only consider ciliary beating as a flow mechanism
in the zebrafish model. The modeled zebrafish ventricular system is thus
a closed system. 

The following three subsections detail our modeling approach for the three
types of flow mechanisms outlined above. For a complete specification of
the resulting boundary conditions, see~\Cref{sec:boundary_conditions}.

\subsubsection{Brain ventricles deformation}\label{subsubsec:deformation}
We model CSF flow driven by ventricular wall deformations associated with
the cardiac cycle by imposing a wall displacement velocity
as boundary condition when solving the flow equations.
The displacement velocity is obtained through a smooth extension of experimental
brain tissue displacement data by solving time-dependent equations of
linear elasticity. We define the displacement of the brain
ventricles as $\ww(\xx, t)$ and the displacement velocity
$\dot{\ww}(\xx, t)\coloneqq\pdifft{\ww}(\xx, t)$. The brain ventricles 
are assumed to be a linear elastic solid, with viscoelastic
behavior modeled by Rayleigh damping. Displacement boundary
conditions based on experimental data are imposed on parts of the boundary,
while the rest of the boundary is considered traction-free.
The dynamic equations are solved to yield
$\ww(\xx, t)$, from which we calculate $\dot{\ww}(\xx, t)$
for use as a boundary condition in the CSF flow model.

Assuming that the brain ventricles are a linear elastic material
with Rayleigh damping, the displacement $\ww(\xx, t)$ is governed by
\begin{equation}
    \rho\ddot{\ww} + \eta_M\rho\dot{\ww} - \eta_K\nabla\cdot\bsigma_s(\dot{\ww})
    - \nabla\cdot\bsigma_s(\ww) = \bm{0} \quad\mathrm{in \ \mathcal{D}},
    \label{eq:strong_linear_elasticity_1}
\end{equation}
where the damping factors $\eta_M$ and $\eta_K$ are
related to the Rayleigh damping factor $\zeta = \eta_M / (2\omega) + \eta_K\omega / 2$
for some eigenfrequency $\omega$. Moreover,
$\bsigma_s = 2\eta\beps(\ww) + \lambda\mathrm{tr}(\beps(\ww))\delta$ is the
solid stress tensor, in which $\eta$ and $\lambda$ are the Lamé parameters,
and $\delta$ is the Kronecker delta. The damping factor $\zeta$ is composed of two separate
damping factors; one related to inertia and one related to stiffness.

For \Cref{eq:strong_linear_elasticity_1}, we partition the boundary 
$\partial\DD$ into three parts: $\partial\DD=\Bcal_1\cup\Bcal_2\cup\Bcal_3$.
We impose the boundary conditions
\begin{alignat}{2}
    \ww(\xx, t) &= \bm{g}(\xx, t) &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \Bcal_1}, \\
    \ww(\xx, t) &= \bm{0} &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \Bcal_2}, \\
    \bsigma_s\nn &= \bm{0} &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \Bcal_3},
\end{alignat}
where $\bm{g}(\xx, t)$ is determined from experimental
data. The boundary $\Bcal_1$ and imposed data consist of:
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
    \item The roof of the lateral ventricles, where corpus callosum displacement data is imposed;
    \item The lateral sides of the frontal horns, where caudate nucleus displacement data is imposed;
    \item The occipital and posterior horns, where we impose a systolic contraction followed by a
    diastolic expansion;
    \item The walls of the third ventricle, where thalamus displacement data is imposed;
    \item The floors of the lateral and third ventricles, where we impose a downward motion.
\end{enumerate}
For (i)--(iv) we impose motion normal to the boundary, while in (v) only motion in the
$z$ direction (which aligns with the feet-head direction) is imposed.
The function $\bm{g}(\xx, t)$ was set determined with experimental
data~\cite{Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius,
Enzmann1992BrainImaging, soellinger20093d, soellinger2007assessment,
greitz1992pulsatile, almudayni2023magnetic, pahlavian2018regional}. 
For some of the experimental data, directly applying the data produced
deformations that resulted in unphysically large amounts of CSF flow.
Therefore, we reduced the magnitudes of some of the displacements,
while retaining the qualitative behavior
of a downward and contractile motion during systole and an upward
and dilated motion during diastole. Reduction of the magnitudes was 
calibrated by comparing the CSF flow results with experimental values
of aqueductal cumulative flow volume\cite{eide2021direction, wagshul2011pulsating}. 

The boundary $\Bcal_2$ consists of the spinal canal outlet
and the lateral apertures. These boundaries are anchored for two reasons:
First, to avoid rigid body motions when solving~\Cref{eq:strong_linear_elasticity_1};
Second, because these boundaries are
considered open boundaries in the fluid model, the displacement velocity
$\dot{\ww}$ on this boundary must be zero to avoid conflicting boundary conditions
between the solid and the fluid model.
Finally, the boundary $\Bcal_3$ is traction-free in all directions. These
parts are the remaining parts of the geometry not mentioned, and certain small
regions delineating different displacement BC regions, to avoid stress concentrations.

The Lamé parameters $\eta$ and $\lambda$ are derived from
Poisson's ratio $\nu$ and Young's modulus $E$ of brain tissue.
In reality the brain ventricles are filled with CSF, and only the
ventricular walls are brain tissue. Since the purpose of the solid model
is to generate a smooth displacement field for the ventricular wall deformations,
we expect the precise choice of $\nu$ and $E$ not to
influence the general trends of the results, but rather
only the required magnitude of the applied displacements $\bm{g}(\xx, t)$,
Moreover, there is uncertainty in these material parameters. Especially $E$, 
for which values reported in the literature span several orders of magnitude
\cite{Budday2015Mechanical, Cheng2010ComputationalModel,
Cheng2007UnconfinedCompression, Sweetman2011ThreeDimensional}.
We adopt the choices of $\nu=0.479$ and $E=1500$
from Causemann~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Causemann2022HumanFramework}
in their computational model of pulsatile CSF flow. The $\nu$ value 
represents a near incompressibility of brain tissue and the ventricular system,
while the $E$ value represents an average of
gray and white matter brain tissue surrounding the ventricular system
\cite{Budday2015Mechanical}. The mass and stiffness damping factors
$\eta_M = 0.20$ and $\eta_K = 0.10$ were set to dampen the motion
such as to control vibrations around the cardiac cycle frequency 1 Hz,
which is close to some of the eiqenfrequencies of the geometry.

\subsubsection{Choroid plexus CSF production}\label{subsubsec:secretion}
CSF production is modeled by imposing a normal flux at the
choroid plexus boundaries. We assume that all CSF that enters the brain ventricles
is produced by the choroid plexus, and that the influx is evenly distributed
over the production sites in the lateral, third and fourth ventricles.
We assume a total daily CSF production
$Q = 504$ ml/day~\cite{Czosnyka2004CerebrospinalDynamics, huff2017neuroanatomy},
where the minus signs is due to $\nn$ facing out of the domain.
The production $Q$ is imposed through a normal flux at the choroid plexus boundary
boundary $\Scal_2$. Since we assume even distribution at the different
choroid plexuses, we impose 
\begin{equation}
    \uu\cdot\nn = -\Tilde{Q} + \dot{\ww}\cdot\nn,
    \label{eq:choroid_plexus_BC}
\end{equation}
where $\Tilde{Q} = \frac{Q}{S}$ and
$S$ is the total choroid plexus boundary surface area.

\subsubsection{Cilia forces}\label{subsubsec:cilia}
Motile cilia are modeled by imposing on the fluid a
tangential traction $\TT_{\parallel} = P_{\nn}(\TT\nn)
= \btau(\xx, t) = \tau P_{\nn}(\rr(\xx, t))$
that represents the collective beating motion of cilia.
Here $P_{\nn}(\rr) = (\mathbb{I} - \nn\otimes\nn)\rr$
is the projection of a vector onto a plane normal
to the boundary normal vector $\nn\coloneqq\nn(\xx, t)$,
while $\tau$ is a constant that determines the 
magnitude of $\btau$. We model the direction $\rr$
motivated by the work of Guirao~\emph{et al.},
who found that motile-cilia beating direction is oriented by
CSF production during neural development~\cite{Guirao2010CouplingCilia}.
To generate the vector $\rr(\xx, t)$, we simulate CSF flow with the
choroid plexus boundary condition~\Cref{eq:choroid_plexus_BC},
a zero normal traction condition on the spinal canal and lateral apertures,
and $\uu\cdot\nn=0$ on the rest of the boundary. In the
tangential direction, we impose the Navier
slip condition $\TT_{\parallel}=-\alpha(\uu\cdot\bm{t})$.
The resulting velocity field,
$\uu_{\mathrm{prod}}$, is normalized by its Euclidean length
to yield the direction of the cilia:
\begin{equation*}
    \rr(\xx, t) = \frac{\uu_{\mathrm{prod}}(\xx, t)}{|\uu_{\mathrm{prod}}(\xx, t)|}.
\end{equation*}

There does not exist accurate data for the parameter $\tau$ that
determines the magnitude of $\btau$, with only a few computational
studies reporting a value for a cilia force magnitude
\cite{Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion, Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles,
Thouvenin2020OriginCanal}.
We set $\tau=7.9$ mPa based on the force magnitude used by
Siyahhan~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles}
for the purpose of comparison,
since they also modeled CSF flow in human brain ventricles.

Based on experimental research, we model cilia everywhere in the 
human brain ventricles, except for in the caudal third of the third
ventricle and on choroid plexus~\cite{doetsch1997cellular,
scott1973scanning, coletti2018characterization, worthington1963ependymal,
scott1972comparative, bruni1972scanning, sanchez2024exploring,
scott1972comparative, ho2023choroid, narita2015cilia}.
In the zebrafish model, cilia are modeled on the dorsal and ventral
parts of the middle ventricle walls, and on the dorsal wall of the anterior
ventricle~\cite{Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment}.

\subsubsection{Boundary condtions for the fluid equations}\label{sec:boundary_conditions}
For the human CSF flow model, we partition the boundary into
three parts: $\partial\DD = \Scal = \Scal_1\cup\Scal_2\cup\Scal_3$.
Using the mechanisms introduced in the previous subsections,
the boundary conditions are
\begin{alignat}{2}
    \uu\cdot\nn &= \dot{\ww}\cdot\nn &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \mathcal{S}_1}, \\
    \TT_{\parallel} &= \tau P_{\nn}(\rr) - \alpha P_{\nn}(\uu)
    &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \mathcal{S}_1\setminus\mathcal{S}_4}, \label{eq:cilia_bc} \\
    \uu\cdot\nn &= -\Tilde{Q} + \dot{\ww}\cdot\nn &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \mathcal{S}_2}, \\
    \TT_{\parallel} &= - \alpha P_{\nn}(\uu) &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \mathcal{S}_2\cup\mathcal{S}_4}, \label{eq:navier_bc} \\
    \TT\nn &= \bm{0} &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \mathcal{S}_3}. \label{eq:outlet_bc}
\end{alignat}
Here $\dot{\ww}(\xx, t)$ is the displacement velocity of the ventricular walls,
the vector $\TT_{\parallel}=\TT\nn - (\TT\nn\cdot\nn)\nn$ is the tangential traction,
$P_{\nn}(\cdot)$ is the projection of a vector onto a plane normal
to the outward-facing normal vector $\nn$. Both \Cref{eq:cilia_bc} and
\Cref{eq:navier_bc} are Navier slip boundary conditions, where the
friction coefficient $\alpha=\mu/L$ is determined by the dynamic viscosity and
the slip length $L$. We model the cilia layer lining the ventricular walls
as a slip layer, such that the velocity slips at the cilia, but becomes
noslip at the underlying wall. We therefore choose $L=15$ \textmu m
which is a typical length of a cilium in human brain ventricles
\cite{Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles,Afzelius2004CiliaRelatedDiseases}
as the slip length, resulting in $\alpha=47 \ \mathrm{Pa\cdot s/m}$.
The boundary $\mathcal{S}_4\subset\mathcal{S}_1$ is the lower third of
the third ventricle, where we do not impose cilia. 
\Cref{eq:outlet_bc} imposes zero traction,
and is imposed on the end of the spinal canal and on the lateral apertures
to simulate open boundaries.

For the zebrafish model, the domain boundary is denoted
$\hat{\Scal} = \hat{\Scal}_1\cup\hat{\Scal}_2$.
Similar to previous work~\cite{Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion},
we consider a frictionless ($\alpha=0$) 
tangential traction on the cilia boundary
and a free-slip condition elsewhere:
\begin{alignat}{2}
    \TT_{\parallel} &= \Tilde{\btau}(\xx, t)  &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \hat{\Scal}_1}, \\
    \TT_{\parallel} &= \bm{0} &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \hat{\Scal}_2}, \\
    \uu\cdot\nn &= 0 &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \hat{\Scal}}.
\end{alignat}
The last condition reflects impermeability of the ventricular walls. The function
$\Tilde{\btau}(\xx, t)$ is strongest in the middle ventricle and weaker in the 
anterior ventricle (\Cref{fig:model_sketch}). See
Herlyng~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion} for more details.

\subsection{Model scenarios and quantities of interest}
To quantify flow and analyze the role of different flow mechanisms
in the human brain model, we investigate three model scenarios:
(i) all three flow mechanisms (deformation, secretion, cilia);
(ii) deformation and cilia, no secretion; (iii) deformation and secretion, no cilia.
To compare results for the different mechanisms, and
also for comparison with previous computational and clinical studies,
we compute the following quantities:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The volumetric CSF flow rate over a cross section in the cerebral aqueduct
    \item The cumulative CSF flow volume in the aqueduct, canal, lateral apertures
    and interventricular foramina.
    \item The CSF stroke volume in the aqueduct, canal, lateral apertures,
    and interventricular foramina. The stroke volume is the maximum of the
    cumulative flow volume across a given cross section.
    \item The peak-to-nadir pressure gradient, measured as the difference in
    the maximum and minimum value of the pressure gradient over a cardiac cycle. 
    \item Maximum and minimum CSF velocities
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Finite-time Lyapunov exponent fields and Lagrangian coherent structures}
We use Finite-time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE) fields to
characterize Lagrangian coherent structures (LCS).
These are material structures of the flow that reveal
how particles are advected by flow over time.
The ridges of the FTLE field are good approximations to
LCS~\cite{shadden2005definition}. LCS can therefore be characterized
by regions of relatively high or low FTLE values.

To compute FTLE fields, we assume that fluid particle trajectories
$\xx(t)\in\mathcal{D}\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ can be described by the
dynamical system
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\xx}(t; t_0, \xx_0) := \frac{\mathrm d\xx}{\mathrm d t} = \uu(\xx(t; t_0, \xx_0), t),
    \label{eq:dx_dt_equals_u}
\end{equation}
where $t$ denotes time, $\uu$ is the CSF velocity, and $\xx_0=\xx(t_0; t_0, \xx_0)$
is the initial position of the particle at the initial time $t_0$. 
\Cref{eq:dx_dt_equals_u} can be integrated to yield the particle position at a time $t>t_0$:
\begin{equation}
    \xx(t; t_0, \xx_0) = \xx(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^t \uu(\xx, s)\,\mathrm ds.
\end{equation}
We define this relation as the mapping
\begin{equation}
    \bphi_{t_0}^t : \mathcal{D} \rightarrow \mathcal{D} : \xx_0 \mapsto \bphi_{t_0}^t(\xx_0) = \xx(t; t_0, \xx_0),
\end{equation}
where $\bphi_{t_0}^t$ is the flow map~\cite{shadden2005definition}.
We have thus far emphasized the dependence of 
the dynamical system and the flow map on the initial configuration $\xx_0$, but will in the 
following omit this notation for brevity. Using the gradient of the flow map,
we define the (right) Cauchy-Green deformation tensor
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{C} = \left(\nabla\bphi_{t_0}^T\right)^*\nabla\bphi_{t_0}^T
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{A}^*$ denotes the transpose of a tensor $\mathbf{A}$
and $T$ is the integration time. 
In the flow field $\uu(\xx, t)$, the maximum stretching that occurs between
two points $\xx$ and $\yy$ aligns with the eigenvector associated with
the maximum eigenvalue $\lambda_{\mathrm{max}}$ of $\mathbf{C}$~\cite{shadden2005definition}.
Related to this eigenvalue is the finite-time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE), defined as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{t_0}^T(\xx) = \frac{1}{\vert T\vert}\log{\sqrt{\lambda_{\mathrm{max}}}}.
\end{equation}
By definition, the FTLE measures the separation of fluid particle trajectories with time:
\begin{equation}
    \max||\delta\xx(\xx, t+t_0)|| = \exp\left(\sigma_{t_0}^T(\xx)|T|\right)||\overline{\delta\xx}(\xx, t_0)||,
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\delta\xx}(\xx, t_0)$ is the particle position change along
the eigenvector associated with $\lambda_{\mathrm{max}}$.
Note the absolute value of the integration time $T$,
which is employed because FTLE values can be calculated
both by integration forward ($T>t_0$) and backward ($T<t_0$) in time.
FTLE values associated with integration forward in time
reveal repelling structures (unstable manifolds) of the flow,
whereas integration backward in time 
reveal attracting structures (stable manifolds).

\subsection{Numerical methods}
\subsubsection{FTLE field computation}
To compute FTLE fields, we initiate a cartesian grid of particles at a
time $t_0$ and compute the flow map $\phi_{t_0}^{t_0+T}$
by numerical integration with a fourth-order Runge-Kutta scheme.
For integration, the timestep size $\Delta t = 0.001$
was chosen to satisfy the CFL condition
$\Delta t \leq C h_{\mathrm{min}}/u_{\mathrm{max}}$,
where $C=0.5$, $h_{\mathrm{min}}$ is the minimal edge length
of the mesh, and $u_{\mathrm{max}}$ is
the maximum velocity magnitude.
Computation is parallelized, uses a local element search technique
to interpolate the velocity field, and only loads velocity data
at the current timestep to minimize memory load. Further details on the
method is provided in previous work~\cite{shadden2010computational}.

\subsubsection{Finite element method for the fluid equations}
We discretize the governing equations of fluid motion
(\Cref{eq:navier_stokes_mom_eq,eq:continuity_eq,eq:time_dependent_stokes_eq,eq:stokes_eq})
with an $\bm{H}(\mathrm{div}; \DD)$-conforming finite element method
using $\mathrm{BDM}_2-\mathrm{DG}_1$ elements for velocity-pressure;
quadratic Brezzi--Douglas--Marini (BDM) elements for the velocity and
linear discontinuous Galerkin (DG)
elements for the pressure. The scheme conserves mass exactly pointwise.
The velocity component normal to facets is continuous
for the BDM elements, while tangential continuity is achieved
by a stabilization technique~\cite{Hong2016AEquations}. 
When solving the Navier-Stokes equations, the convective term
is linearized as $(\uu^{n+1}\cdot\nabla)\uu^{n+1} \approx (\uu^n\cdot\nabla)\uu^{n+1}$
where $n$ denotes timestep. Moreover, the convective fluxes both on facets
internal to the mesh and on open boundaries are stabilized with
an upwinding technique~\cite{Schroeder2018Towards,
di2011mathematical}.

The cilia tangential traction boundary condition is imposed weakly,
while the ventricular wall displacement velocity is imposed strongly.
The choroid plexus flux boundary
condition is imposed weakly using Nitsche's method
\cite{Nitsche1971UberSind, benzaken2024constructing}.
A detailed derivation
of the BDM--DG discretization for the time-dependent 
Stokes equations can be found in
Herlyng~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion}.

In time, the fluid equations are discretized with the first-order, implicit
backward Euler scheme using a timestep $\Delta t = 0.001$ s.
Since we use an implicit time discretization and solve the fluid euquations
directly, an initial condition is only needed
for the velocity field, and not the pressure.
When solving the Stokes equations, the fluid is 
initially at rest: $\uu_0(\xx)=\uu(\xx, 0)=\bm{0}$.
For the Navier-Stokes equations, we solve the Stokes equations
(with $\uu_0(\xx)=\bm{0}$) and use the solution
$\uu_{\mathrm{Stokes}}(\xx)$ as initial condition;
$\uu_0(\xx)=\uu_{\mathrm{Stokes}}(\xx)$. 
The fluid equations are solved for two cardiac cycles,
and the velocities $\uu(\xx, t)$ of the second cycle are used
to calculate FTLE fields. Because the FTLE computation code 
interpolates velocity based on values in the mesh nodes, the
finite element velocity approximation is projected from $\mathrm{BDM}_2$
into $\mathrm{CG}_2$ and then evaluated in a $\mathrm{CG}_1$ space.

\subsubsection{Finite element method for the solid equations}
The time-dependent linear elasticity equations (\Cref{eq:strong_linear_elasticity_1})
are solved using a finite element method with
fourth-order continuous Lagrange elements for the
spatial discretization. In time, the
equations are discretized with the Newmark-$\beta$ method,
for which updates of the displacement $\ww$ and 
displacement velocity $\dot{\ww}$ are
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \ww_{n+1} &= \ww_n + \Delta t\dot{\ww}_n + \left(\frac{1}{2} - \beta\right)\Delta t^2\ddot{\ww}_n + \beta\Delta t^2\ddot{\ww}_{n+1}, \\
        \dot{\ww}_{n+1} &= \dot{\ww}_n + \left(1 - \gamma\right)\Delta t\ddot{\ww}_n + \gamma\Delta t\ddot{\ww}_{n+1},
    \end{aligned}
    \label{eq:newmark_beta_method}
\end{equation}
where $n$ denotes timestep and $\Delta t = 0.001$ is the timestep size,
while $\ddot{\ww}_n$ is the acceleration.
We set $\gamma=1/2$ and $\beta=1/4$, which yields
a second-order accurate and unconditionally
stable scheme~\cite{newmark1959method}.
With this choice of $\gamma$ and $\beta$, there is no
numerical damping in the Newmark-$\beta$ method.
We observed high-frequency noise in the displacement velocity $\dot{\ww}$ 
calculated with~\Cref{eq:newmark_beta_method}, which is
an issue that can arise when solving problems with prescribed displacement
boundary conditions~\cite{bathe2012insight}. Therefore,
to ensure smoothness of $\dot{\ww}$ before
applying it as a boundary condition in the fluid model,
we computed $\dot{\ww}$ by Fourier transformation
of the displacement $\ww$ and differentiation in the frequency space.

The linear elasticity equations are solved for four cardiac cycles,
and the displacement velocities computed in the fourth cycle
are used as boundary conditions in the fluid equations.
Starting the system from rest with initial conditions
$\ww(\xx, 0) = \dot{\ww}(\xx, 0) = \ddot{\ww}(\xx, 0) = \bm{0}$
and smoothly transitioning to the full periodic boundary data
is important to achieve stable results, because
an impulsive start could introduce non-physical oscillations. % cite
To ensure this, a cosine window is applied
to the boundary condition magnitudes during the first half of
the initial cardiac cycle. Additionally, we perform a ramp-up
of the magnitudes over the first two cardiac cycles.
Traction-free conditions were imposed weakly, while feet-head
displacements were imposed strongly. The normal displacements
were imposed weakly using Nitsche's method~\cite{Nitsche1971UberSind,
benzaken2024constructing}.

\subsection{FTLE fields demonstrate ventricular flow compartmentalization in zebrafish embryos}
Experimental and computational studies have demonstrated compartmentalization of
brain ventricular flow in embryonic zebrafish ventricles~\cite{Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment,
Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion}. We therefore investigate structures in FTLE fields
in the interventricular duct connecting the anterior and middle ventricles.
The repelling and attracting structures combine to create a separating
structure (\Cref{fig:FTLE_zfish}). This structure indicates compartmentalization of flow and confines
the CSF to the respective ventricles, obstructing interventricular flow. 
The integration time $T$ required to reveal these structures is more than one
order of magnitude larger than the $T$ values used for the human ventricles.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Lagrangian structures are present in the ventricular system}
Both in the zebrafish and the human model, we find ridges in the finite-time
Lyapunov exponent fields (FTLE) that indicate the presence
of Lagrangian coherent structures (LCS). The jet observed in 
the third ventricle resonates well with previous computational
studies~\cite{Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius}. The time-dependent FTLE
fields indicate that the jet creates a vortex every cardiac cycle. 
The repelling structures have a well-defined ridge when $T=1.0$ s
that separates the third ventricle from the aqueduct, which means
that once fluid has reached the entrance to the aqueduct, it is quickly
expelled through the aqueduct. Meanwhile, fluid inside the third ventricle
can stay there for a longer time without exiting through the aqueduct.

In the interventricular foramina, there are also
clear ridges in the FTLE field with $T=1.0$ s, indicating regions of high stretching
and delineation of regions with different advective transport times.
The lobes that develop have a structure that could trap fluid close to the ventricular
walls. 

In the zebrafish ventricles FTLE fields, there are clearly defined structures,
both attracting and repelling, in the interventricular duct separating the
anterior and middle ventricles. If one for a given integration time $T$ 
combines the attracting and repelling FTLE fields, the prominent structure forms
a hyperbolic separation point. The separation point confines flow to
separate parts of the ventricular system. This finding aligns well with
previous studies that have demonstrated flow
compartmentalization in embryonic zebrafish
brain ventricles~\cite{Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment,
Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion}.

\subsection{The effects of fluid inertia on ventricular flow and transport}
The results we present indicate that when simulating CSF flow
in human ventricles at Reynolds numbers in the lower hundreds, accounting for intertial effects through
solving the full Navier-Stokes equations is important when investigating
advective transport by the CSF flow, as well as analysis of local flow features.
We draw this conclusion based on the vast differences in the structures
present in the FTLE fields and the differences observed in local flow features
between the Navier-Stokes and Stokes solutions. The lobe structures that 
form in the Navier-Stokes FTLE fields indicate stronger stirring and mixing of
CSF than in the CSF flow approximated with the Stokes equations.

In terms of computing macroscopic or integrated quantities like stroke volume,
however, the Stokes equations seem sufficiently accurate, showing little discrepancy with
the Navier-Stokes solution. As such, the Stokes equations can be
utilized as a low-cost alternative for fast predictions
in computational frameworks where integrated quantities are needed.
We note that several previous computational studies report maximal aqueductal
CSF velocities on a mm/s scale~\cite{enzmann1993cerebrospinal, enzmann1991normal,
Causemann2022HumanFramework, Sweetman2011ThreeDimensional, howden2008three, wagshul2011pulsating,
greitz1993pulsatile, sweetman2011cerebrospinal}, which is ten times lower than the
cm/s scale reported here and in several other works
\cite{kim1999quantitative, nitz1992flow, Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius}. 
This ten-fold reduction in maximal velocity would reduce the Reynolds number
by a factor ten. Although inertial forces would still dominate viscous forces at 
these numbers, investigating the effects on the discrepancy in the advective transport
between the Stokes and Navier-Stokes solutions would be interesting to address in future work.

\subsection{The effects of motile cilia and CSF production}
Disregarding motile cilia did not alter the flow in the human brain ventricles significantly
in terms of macroscopic flow quantities like stroke volume. 
There were however minor differences in the FTLE field structures when disregarding cilia,
with the lobe structures near the aqueduct entrance more attached to the 3V wall when including cilia,
indicating that cilia alter the near-wall flow.
This finding compares well with the computational study by Siyahhan~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles},
whose results showed only a minor effect of cilia on near-wall flow.
On the other hand, in the model of Yoshida~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Yoshida2022EffectVentricles} 
cilia had a significant effect on mixing. We observed that the cilia model parameters
strongly influenced the effect of cilia on the flow (see \Cref{subsec:limitations}),
and we are of the opinion that any further insights on the effect of cilia in
brain ventricular flow and transport would heavily benefit from more experimental data
on cilia parameters in the human brain ventricles.
This contrasts the case for the embryonic zebrafish brain, where the model parameters
are more comprehensively resolved, and previous work strongly suggests cilia are
the main drivers of flow~\cite{Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment, Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion}.
The great effect of cilia in zebrafish ventricles is a direct result of the smaller
length scale of the ventricular system of embryonic zebrafish
as compared to adult humans. Cilia may play a similarly important
role in smaller confined spaces of the human ventricular system,
such as in the inferior and occipital horns of the lateral ventricles.

The constant-in-time CSF production we imposed had little impact, apart from
generating a net outward flow from the ventricular system. In terms of local
flow features, there was no noticeable difference when disregarding CSF production.
The FTLE fields were also largely unaffected compared to the baseline model.

\subsection{Model validation: brain deformations and ventricular CSF flow}
To ensure that the CSF flow we modeled to be a result of cardiac pulsatility 
brain deformations had a physiological magnitude,
the stroke volume (cumulative flow volume) in the cerebral aqueduct was used to
calibrate the deformation boundary conditions such that
the deformations yielded flow volumes (when solving the
Navier-Stokes equations) similar to those
reported by Eide~\emph{et al.}~\cite{eide2021direction}.
This choice was made based on the fact that the 
ventricular volume displacement directly determines
the volume of displaced CSF.
We also compared other quantities, such as the peak-to-nadir pressure
gradient, with previously reported values.
We note that some of these quantities, vary widely
in terms of values reported in research literature.

Eide~\emph{et al.}~\cite{eide2021direction} reported values of $67\pm 57$ \textmu l
for the aqueductal stroke volume, which is in the higher range of reported values~\cite{baledent2004relationship,
Liu2024UsingBreathing, kim1999quantitative},
while some studies report lower values in the range
of 15--30 \textmu l~\cite{wagshul2011pulsating, nitz1992flow, greitz1994mr,
greitz1993pulsatile}.
We therefore calibrated the deformations by tuning down the displacement magnitude,
such that the aqueductal stroke volume
computed to 46.1\textmu l, a value in the lower range of those reported by Eide~\emph{et al.}.
With this choice, the resulting pressure gradient matches well with that
reported by Eide~\emph{et al.}~\cite{eide2021direction},
and also other experimental studies~\cite{Liu2024UsingBreathing, ringstad2017non}.
Also, the volumetric flow rates are in the range of clinically
reported values~\cite{Liu2024UsingBreathing, wagshul2011pulsating}.
The resulting maximum and minimum velocity magnitudes
of our model are slightly greater than those reported by Eide~\emph{et al.}~\cite{eide2021direction},
and also other studies that report velocities on the scale of centimeters per second
\cite{kim1999quantitative, nitz1992flow, Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius}. We note
however that several previous studies, both in experiment and computational studies,
report millimeter per second velocities~\cite{enzmann1993cerebrospinal, enzmann1991normal,
Causemann2022HumanFramework, Sweetman2011ThreeDimensional, howden2008three, wagshul2011pulsating,
greitz1993pulsatile, sweetman2011cerebrospinal}.

We note that the deformations in our solid model are smaller in magnitude than the experimental 
data they are based on, although the qualitative behavior of the displacement curves were conserved.
The magnitude of the maximum volume change in our model was around 0.15\%. This is a lot less
than that reported by previous research, where 0.9\%~\cite{zhu2006dynamics}
and even as high as 20\%~\cite{lee1989variation} has been reported. The latter value would
produce unrealistically large flow volumes in our model, but the discrepancy between our model
and experimental reports strongly indicate the absence of another volume-regulating mechanism 
in our model. Absorption of CSF through the ventricular walls could be one such mechanism.

The cephalocaudal displacements we imposed are well-documented~\cite{Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius,
Enzmann1992BrainImaging, greitz1992pulsatile, soellinger2007assessment, soellinger20093d}.
There is more uncertainty regarding displacements in the medial-lateral direction. Our understanding is
that arterial swelling exerts pressure on the parenchyma and results in
squeezing of the ventricular system, a squeezing which seems to be symmetric
in the horizontal plane, as symmetry characterizes medial-lateral motion
in some experimental reports~\cite{soellinger20093d, abderezaei2021development}. It has been
reported that the periventricular deformations could be related to
deformations in the third and lateral ventricles~\cite{abderezaei2021development}.
We imposed medial-lateral displacements on the third and lateral 
ventricles based on these reported findings, and the fact that our model
was unable to produce physiological CSF flow without these displacements;
when imposing only cephalocaudal displacements, the CSF flowrates were less than
10\% of experimentally reported values. This resonates well with the conclusion of
Kurtcuoglu~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius},
namely that medial-lateral displacement
of the third ventricle is required to fully explain 
the observed flowrates of ventricular CSF flow.

We considered the beating direction of cilia to align with the direction of 
CSF production velocities, similar to Siyahhan~\emph{et al.}, who considered
directionality of the cilia forcing aligned
with the wall shear stress of the macroscopic flow simulated in a
production-only setup~\cite{Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles}.
In our setup, with the only free boundaries of the geometry located
in the caudal end of the geometry, the production velocity and thus
the ciliary beating is generally caudally directed. Assessing the impact
of this directionality, and whether e.g. a more cephalic directed beating
influences the results, could be investigated in future work.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{subsec:limitations}
This modeling work comprises several mechanisms, parameters and
assumptions that are uncertain.
For the solid equations we used a damped linear elastic model,
where there is an inherent
uncertainty in the material parameters
of brain tissue~\cite{smillie2005hydroelastic}.
Biological soft tissues rarely exhibit Hookean material behavior,
and nonlinear models are presumably most appropriate to
model deformations over longer timespans
\cite{Cheng2010ComputationalModel, Cheng2007UnconfinedCompression,
smillie2005hydroelastic, tully2009coupling, kaczmarek1997hydromechanics}.
We justify our choice of deformation model by the fact that
we seek simply a smooth extension to the rest of the domain
for the displacement measurement boundary condition $\bm{g}(\xx, t)$,
while the ventricles are in reality fluid-filled cavities.
The choice of linear elasticity with linear damping is considered
adequate since we consider deformations on the timescale of a cardiac cycle,
where nonlinear damping effects are negligible.

In terms of the flow model, there are uncertainties related to several mechanisms.
There is great uncertainty around our choice of Navier slip boundary condition.
First, the force per surface area parameter $\tau$ is based on the value used by
Siyahhan~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles}, which they obtained by
calibrating CSF flow simulations with rodent CSF velocity data. Because the
ependymal cells lining the human brain ventricular walls
in general are ciliated to a higher degree than in the rodent brain~\cite{spassky2017development},
the ciliary beating might be stronger in human brain ventricles
than the beating in the rodent brain. Second,
the friction coefficient $\alpha=47 \ \mathrm{Pa\cdot s/m}$
is estimated by assuming a slip length equal to the depth of the cilia layer lining
the wall. This was chosen partly by comparing the cilia-generated velocities for
different values of $\alpha$. Setting $\alpha=0$ like in the zebrafish model,
the velocities exceeded 1 cm/s, which we considered large compared to measured
values and our current understanding of ventricular flow.
On the other hand, setting the higher value $\alpha=1000 \ \mathrm{Pa\cdot s/m}$, which is
the value that Causemann~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Causemann2022HumanFramework}
employed for their Beavers-Joseph-Saffman slip condition at the ventricular
walls, modeling the walls as porous with transport into the neighboring
parenchyma, the cilia-generated velocities were almost absent. This is because
$\alpha=1000 \ \mathrm{Pa\cdot s/m}$ is approaching a noslip regime,
where our cilia tangential traction would break. The resulting
particle paths generated by our model would then not compare well with
\emph{in vivo} data~\cite{stadlbauer2010insight}. We therefore
chose the value of $\alpha=47 \ \mathrm{Pa\cdot s/m}$ as a 
compromise between these two cases. An experiment looking into
fair values of both $\tau$ and $\alpha$ is required for further
insights on this.

% Need citations in the following paragraph
Moreover, CSF production might in reality neither be constant nor evenly
distributed across the choroid plexuses of the ventricles~\cite{}.
Also, in reality 10--30\% of the CSF production
may come from interstitial fluid transported through the ependymal cell
layer of the brain ventricles walls into the ventricles~\cite{Brodal2016TheSystem}.
Additionally, there is probably absorption of CSF through the ventricular walls
into the brain parenchyma~\cite{}, which we did not model.
Also, respiration has a flow component~\cite{Vinje2019RespiratoryMeasurements},
while we only modeled pulsatile deformations driven by a cardiac freqeuncy.
Here, we disregarded these (and other possible mechanisms) to focus
on the impact of cilia and CSF production on the ventricular flow and transport.

\section{Conclusions and outlook}
We have presented a computational framework for using finite-time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE)
fields to characterize Lagrangian coherent structures in brain ventricles.
The FTLE fields are computed from velocity fields generated with finite element simulations
of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow. We demonstrated that accounting for inertial effects
has little importance when computing integrated flow quantities, but that the inertial
effects are crucial for capturing Lagrangian coherent structures and thus crucial when
modeling transport and mixing in the (human) brain ventricles. Through comparison with previous
Eulerian-based flow analysis of CSF flow in embryonic zebrafish brain ventricles, our results further
demonstrate how FTLE fields can be used to characterize prominent flow structures in brain ventricles.
There are still several unresolved model parameters and assumptions, but hopefully this work inspires
future work on Lagrangian analysis of brain ventricular flow and transport.


Reportedly, quantitation of aqueductal CSF flow as a function
of the cardiac cycle may enhance our ability to diagnose one of the
potentially treatable causes of dementia, namely normal 
pressure hydrocephalus~\cite{bradley1986flowing}. Looking at how
geometry modifications impact LCS in the brain ventricles
and the aqueduct could add insights to this.

\section{Model verification}
Correctness of the zebrafish CSF flow model implementation
was verified in previous work~\cite{Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion},
thus we only present verification analysis of the human brain model here.
Mesh and polynomial order refinement studies for the human
model were performed both for the fluid and the solid equations.
We refine element polynomial degree on a single mesh.
We also refine mesh with a single element degree.
We consider three versions of the ventricles mesh,
with a total of 51 016 (maximal 0.73 cm, minimal 0.049 cm), 408 128 (maximal 0.40 cm, minimal 0.023 cm),
and 3 265 024 (maximal 0.22 cm, minimal 0.010 cm) tetrahedral cells.
We look at convergence in the maximum displacement magnitude, the
displacement in a point on the traction-free boundary, and
the kinetic and elastic eneries.

Cardiac cycle independecy was assessed. We simulated flow for
2 cardiac cycles and deformation for 4 cardiac cycles. Simulating flow for 4
instead of 2 cardiac cycles showed made virtually no change to most of the computed flow
quantities, with the biggest discrepancy being a 1.5\% change in the max-to-min pressure
gradient, verifying periodicity. The displacement results were also unchanged when simulating
for more than 4 cardiac cycles. These results justify our choice.

Correctness of the discretization schemes on the human brain ventricles geometry
were verified using the Method of Manufactured Solutions~\cite{Roache2001CodeSolutions}.
Convergence properties of the fluid discretization scheme were assessed with two
model problems. To assess spatial convergence, we solved a channel flow
problem, observing linear convergence in both the velocity error measured
in the $\bm{H}^1$ semi-norm and the pressure error measured in the $L^2$ norm.
Temporal convergence was assessed
with a Taylor vortex problem~\cite{Karniadakis2005Verification},
where the first-order accuracy of the backward Euler scheme was
confirmed. Solving the linear elasticity equations numerically
with quadratic basis functions, the errors of the displacement
approximation measured in the $\bm{L}^2$ norm and
$\bm{H}^1$ semi-norm converged at the optimal rates of three
and two, respectively. In time, second-order accuracy of the 
Newmark-$\beta$ method was confirmed.
