\section{Introduction}
% I1: What is the problem - from big picture to our precise topic - why is it interesting?
Our brains are constantly in motion. Within the brain reside hollow structures called
the brain ventricles, wherein cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flows dynamically.
The CSF flow is an important contributor to brain homeostasis
and the regulatory processes of the brain. Volume transmission is a theory that outlines
the importance of CSF flow in the neuromodulatory processes of the brain. Studying
the flow topology in brain ventricles bears interest as it could help understand
transport of vital solutes and particles.

% I2: What do we know about the problem and the topic?
The underlying mechanisms of brain ventricular CSF flow have been studied extensively
in the last decades. Still a topic of debate, mounting evidence suggests that
the bulk pulsatile flow is associated with the cardiac and respiratory cycles.
Smaller flow scales are affected by beating of motile cilia lining the ventricular 
walls. Neural activity has also been shown to impact CSF flow patterns.
Simple visualizations of flow trajectories in animals have shown that
ventricular cerebrospinal fluid flow has intricate details,
with demonstrations of stagnation and separation points
and vortex-structured flows \cite{gilpin2017flowtrace,
Faubel2016CiliaVentricles,
Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment}.
Lagrangian Coherent Structures (LCS) have proved themselves to be 
an informative way of studying advective transport in fluid flows
\cite{shadden2005definition, haller2015lagrangian}. LCS are 
defining structures of fluid flows that delineate regions
of maximal and minimal stretching, and LCS are the transport
topology skeleton of the flow. Previous studies
have used LCS approximation to increase understanding of mixing 
and flow structures in blood flow~\cite{shadden2008characterization},
atmospheric flow~\cite{Gunther2021LagrangianWinds},
airway flow~\cite{lukens2010using},
oceanic flows, ... etc. One way of characterizing LCS is the
finite-time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE) field of the velocity. Ridges
of the FTLE field are good approximations of the LCS of the 
flow~\cite{shadden2005definition}.

% I3: What do we not know about the problem and what do we want to
% know? What are the precise research questions we want to address?
% Refer to specific studies and what specifically we do not know.
Flow and mixing mechanisms in the human central nervous system
and specifically the brain ventricles
are still not completely understood.
Many previous studies are based on Eulerian flow analysis,
looking at instantaneous flow variables such as velocity.
These instantaneous variables may however deceive
interpretation of time-dependence~\cite{shadden2005definition}. 
We therefore want to study brain ventricular flow
from a Lagrangian persepctive. Specifically, we
characterize LCS in brain ventricles,
with the aim of gaining further
insights on flow and mixing in the ventricles, which impacts
CSF composition and thus neuromodulatory processes.

% I4: How do we address the problem and what do we find out?
In this work, we study intraventricular CSF flow in embryonic zebrafish and adult human brain ventricles.
The geometries are segmented from medical imaging data. First, we simulate CSF flow using a finite
element method. The CSF velocity fields are used to analyze flow topology using particle
trajectories and finite-time Lyapunov exponent fields.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Finite-time Lyapunov exponent fields}
Consider the dynamical system
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\xx}(t; t_0, \xx_0) = \frac{\mathrm d\xx}{\mathrm d t} = \uu(\xx(t; t_0, \xx_0), t),
    \label{eq:dx_dt_equals_u}
\end{equation}
where $\xx(t)\in$ are the spatial coordinates of the domain $\mathcal{D}\subset\mathbb{R}^d$,
$t$ denotes time, $\uu$ is a velocity, and $\xx_0=\xx(t_0; t_0, \xx_0)$ is the initial
position at the initial time $t_0$. 
\Cref{eq:dx_dt_equals_u} can be integrated to yield the position at a time $T$:
\begin{equation}
    \xx(T) = \xx(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^T \uu(\xx, t)\,\mathrm d t.
\end{equation}
We define this relation as the mapping
\begin{equation}
    \bphi_{t_0}^t : \mathcal{D} \rightarrow \mathcal{D} : \xx_0 \mapsto \bphi_{t_0}^t(\xx_0) = \xx(t; t_0, \xx_0),
\end{equation}
where $\bphi_{t_0}^t$ is the flow map~\cite{shadden2005definition}.
We have thus far emphasized the dependence of 
the dynamical system and the flow map on the parameters $t_0, \xx_0$, but will in the 
following omit this notation for brevity. Using the gradient of the flow map,
we define the (right) Cauchy-Green deformation tensor
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{C} = \left(\nabla\bphi_{t_0}^T\right)^*\nabla\bphi_{t_0}^T
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{A}^*$ denotes the transpose of a tensor $\mathbf{A}$
and $T$ is the integration time. 
In the flow field $\uu(\xx, t)$, the maximum stretching that occurs between
two points $\xx$ and $\yy$ aligns with the eigenvector associated with
the maximum eigenvalue $\lambda_{\mathrm{max}}$ of $\mathbf{C}$~\cite{shadden2005definition}.
Related to this eigenvalue is the finite-time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE), defined as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{t_0}^T(\xx) = \frac{1}{\vert T\vert}\log{\sqrt{\lambda_{\mathrm{max}}}}.
\end{equation}
By definition, the FTLE measures the separation of fluid particle trajectories with time:
\begin{equation}
    \max||\delta\xx(\xx, t+t_0)|| = \exp\left(\sigma_{t_0}^T(\xx)|T|\right)||\overline{\delta\xx}(\xx, t_0)||,
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\delta\xx}(\xx, t_0)$ is the particle position change along
the eigenvector associated with $\lambda_{\mathrm{max}}$.
Note the absolute value of the integration time $T$,
which is employed because FTLE values can be calculated
both by integration forward ($T>0$) and backward ($T<0$) in time.
FTLE values associated with integration forward in time
reveal repelling structures (unstable manifolds) of the flow,
whereas integration backward in time 
reveal attracting structures (stable manifolds).

\subsection{The governing equations of cerebrospinal fluid flow}
We consider flow of an incompressible, Newtonian fluid of density $\rho$ and dynamic viscosity
$\mu$ in a domain $\DD\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ with boundary $\partial\DD=\Scal$.
Neglecting the graviational force, no body forces are acting on the fluid.
The flow is governed by the Navier-Stokes equations:
\begin{alignat}{1}
    \rho\frac{\mathrm D\uu}{\mathrm Dt} 
    = \rho\left(\pdifft{\uu}
    + (\uu\cdot\nabla)\uu\right) &= \nabla\cdot\TT,
    \label{eq:navier_stokes_eq} \\
    \nabla\cdot\uu &= 0,
    \label{eq:continuity_eq}
\end{alignat}
with the stress tensor $\TT$ defined as
\begin{equation*}
    \TT = 2\mu\beps(\uu) - p\mathbf{I},
\end{equation*}
where $\uu(\xx, t)\in\bm{H}^1(\DD)$ and $p(\xx, t)\in L_0^2(\DD)$
are the fluid velocity and pressure,
$\mathbf{I}$ is the identity tensor in dimension $d$ and
$\beps(\uu)=\frac{1}{2}\left(\nabla\uu + (\nabla\uu)^T\right)$ is the
strain-rate tensor (or symmetric gradient) of $\uu$. 
\Cref{eq:continuity_eq} is the continuity equation
of an incompressible fluid, which reflects mass conservation.

The domain $\DD$ is deforming and varies over time. To handle this,
we solve the Navier-Stokes equations with an
Arbitrary Eulerian Lagrangian (ALE) approach~\cite{Duarte2004ALE}.
The governing equations are solved in the reference configuration $\chi$,
using the mapping $\psi : \xx\mapsto\chi$ from the current deformed
configuration at time $t$ to the reference configuration.
In this setup, the governing equations are
\begin{alignat}{1}
    \rho\left(\pdifft{\uu} + ((\uu - \cc)\cdot\hat{\nabla})\uu\right)
    &= \hat{\nabla}\cdot\TT \label{eq:ALE_momentum_eq} \\
    \hat{\nabla}\cdot\uu &= 0 \label{eq:ALE_continuity_eq}
\end{alignat}
where $\cc$ is the deformation velocity and
$\hat{\nabla}$ is the differential operator in the deformed domain.
In \Cref{eq:ALE_momentum_eq} and \Cref{eq:ALE_continuity_eq},
all variables are evaluated in the reference configuration.
In this ALE approach, the differential operators
and integration measures of the weak formulation
must be transformed appropriately to account for the
domain motion~\cite{Scovazzi2007LectureNotes}.

For fluid flows where viscous forces dominate inertial 
forces (low Reynolds numbers), the Stokes equations
\begin{equation}
    - \nabla\cdot\TT = \bm{0}
    \label{eq:stokes_eq}
\end{equation}
are often used as an approximation for the momentum equation.
Viscous forces dominate in CSF flow in smaller brains
such as embryonic zebrafish brain ventricles
\cite{Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment, Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion},
and we will therefore use \Cref{eq:stokes_eq} to model CSF flow
in zebrafish. In the human brain, flow measurements and simulations
indicate non-negligible transient and inertial effects 
\cite{Gholampour2018FSI, howden2008three}.
The flow is in a transitional regime where it is not
entirely clear whether the Stokes equations might be a fair
approximation, and previous studies
\cite{Causemann2022HumanFramework} have modeled 
CSF flow in human brain ventricles
by the time-dependent Stokes equations:
\begin{equation}
    \rho\pdifft{\uu} - \nabla\cdot\TT = \bm{0}.
    \label{eq:time_dependent_stokes_eq}
\end{equation}
Here the convective acceleration term
$(\uu\cdot\nabla)\uu$ in the Navier-Stokes equations 
is neglected, while the local acceleration $\rho\partial\uu/\partial t$
is retained to account for transient effects. We will compute
flow in human brain ventricles with both the Navier-Stokes and the
time-dependent Stokes equations to compare the two.

\subsection{Flow mechanisms and boundary conditions}
We consider three fluid motion-inducing mechanisms, and model them as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Deformations of the brain ventricles related to the cardiac and respiratory cycles,
    modeled with an imposed deformation velocity (see~\Cref{subsubsec:deformation})
    \item Secretion of CSF from the choroid plexus,
    modeled with an influx boundary condition on the velocity (see~\Cref{subsubsec:secretion})
    \item Ciliary beating, modeled as a tangential traction acting on the fluid at the boundary (see~\Cref{subsubsec:cilia})
\end{itemize}
We impose zero normal traction at the lateral apertures and the cut-off of the spinal canal
to model them as open boundaries that allow for in- and outflow.

\subsubsection{Brain ventricles deformation}\label{subsubsec:deformation}
To model the impact of ventricular wall deformations associated with the
cardiac and respiratory cycles on CSF flow, we impose a
displacement velocity as a boundary
condition when solving the flow equations.
We define the displacement of the brain
ventricles as $\ww(\xx, t)\in H^2(\DD)$,
and the displacement velocity is
$\partial\ww/\partial t$. To obtain
$\ww(\xx, t)$, we model the brain ventricles 
as a linear elastic solid, impose displacement 
boundary conditions on parts of the geometry,
and solve for $\ww(\xx, t)$ in the rest of the domain.
The displacement is governed by
\begin{equation}
    \rho\pdiffdifft{\ww} - \nabla\cdot\bsigma_s = \bm{0} \quad\mathrm{in \ \mathcal{D}},
    \label{eq:strong_linear_elasticity_1}
\end{equation}
where $\bsigma_s = 2\eta\beps(\ww) + \lambda\mathrm{tr}(\beps(\ww))\delta$ is the
solid stress tensor, in which $\eta$ and $\lambda$ are the Lamé parameters of the ventricular walls
and $\delta$ is the Kronecker delta. Applying the divergence operator, \Cref{eq:strong_linear_elasticity_1}
takes the form
\begin{equation}
    \rho\pdiffdifft{\ww} - 2\eta\nabla\cdot\beps(\ww) - \lambda\nabla(\nabla\cdot\ww) = \bm{0},
    \quad\mathrm{in \ \mathcal{D}}.
\end{equation}
We impose the boundary conditions
\begin{alignat}{2}
    \ww(\xx, t) &= \bm{g}(\xx, t) &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \mathcal{S}_1}, \\
    \bsigma_s\nn &= \bm{0} &&\quad\mathrm{on \ \mathcal{S}_2 = \mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{S}_1}.
\end{alignat}
On $\mathcal{S}_1$ we impose a given deformation $\bm{g}(\xx, t)$, whereas
$\mathcal{S}_2$ is considered traction-free.
The displacement $\bm{g}$ is based on brain displacement
measurements done in the sagittal plane~\cite{Kurtcuoglu2007ComputationalSylvius,
Enzmann1992BrainImaging}, showing displacements
along the feet-head axis ($z$ axis),
and therefore $\bm{g}(\xx, t) = (0, 0, g_z(\xx, t))$.
The Lamé parameters are derived from the Poisson's ratio $\nu=\fixme{0.479}$ and Young's modulus
$E=\fixme{1500}$ Pa of white matter brain tissue that surrounds the ventricles
\cite{Causemann2022HumanFramework}. Although the uncertainty in these material parameters
is high, with the reported values in literature spanning
quite a range~\cite{Cheng2010ComputationalModel, Cheng2007UnconfinedCompression},
we expect a limited impact at least of the choice of $E$ modulus, since
an increase in $E$ had no impact on clinically relevant features such
as displaced flow volume in the computational work of
Causemann~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Causemann2022HumanFramework}.

Biological soft tissues rarely exhibit Hookean material behavior,
and nonlinear models are presumably most appropriate
\cite{Cheng2010ComputationalModel, Cheng2007UnconfinedCompression,
smillie2005hydroelastic, tully2009coupling, kaczmarek1997hydromechanics},
and so the assumption that the brain ventricles is linear-elastic is 
questionable. We nonetheless chose this approach because: (i) the
ventricles are in reality fluid-filled, we
seek simply a smooth extension to the rest of the domain
for the displacement measurement boundary condition $\bm{g}(\xx, t)$; 
(ii) there is anyways still uncertainty in the material parameters
of brain tissue~\cite{smillie2005hydroelastic},
making detailed modeling of it rather arbitrary.

\subsubsection{Choroid plexus secretion}\label{subsubsec:secretion}
CSF is mainly produced by the choroid plexus tissue.
We assume that all CSF that enters the brain ventricles
is produced by the choroid plexus, and that the influx is evenly distributed
over the production sites in the lateral, third and fourth ventricles.
However, in reality 10--30\% of the CSF production
may come from interstitial fluid transported through the ependymal cell
layer of the brain ventricles walls into the ventricles~\cite{Brodal2016TheSystem}.
The CSF secretion is modeled by imposing a normal flux at the
boundary $\Schp$ such that the total flux through the choroid plexus boundary is
\begin{equation}
    \int_{\Schp}(\uu\cdot\nn)\,\mathrm ds = Q.
\end{equation}
The total flux $Q=504$ ml/day is the daily total
CSF production in a human brain~\cite{Czosnyka2004CerebrospinalDynamics}.

\subsubsection{Cilia forces}\label{subsubsec:cilia}
Motile cilia are modeled by imposing forces on the fluid with a
boundary condition on the tangential traction
$P_{\nn}(\bsigmahat) = \btau = \tau P_{\nn}(\rr)$.
Here $\bsigmahat=\bsigma\nn$ is the traction vector and
$P_{\nn}(\cdot)$ is the projection of a vector onto a plane normal
to $\nn$. Work by Guirao \emph{et al.} indicates
that motile cilia beating direction is oriented by
CSF production during neural development~\cite{Guirao2010CouplingCilia}.
This was modeled by Siyahhan \emph{et al.}, who considered
directionality of the cilia forcing aligned
with the wall shear stress of the macroscopic flow simulated in a
production-only setup~\cite{Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles}.
We follow a similar approach, simulating CSF flow with the
boundary condition~\eqref{eq:choroid_plexus_BC} on $\Schp$,
a zero normal traction condition on the spinal canal and lateral apertures,
and $\uu\cdot\nn=0$ on the rest of the boundary. In the
tangential direction, the CSF is free to slip. The resulting velocity field,
$\uu_{\mathrm{prod}}$, is normalized in length to yield the direction of the cilia:
\begin{equation*}
    \rr = \frac{\uu_{\mathrm{prod}}}{|\uu_{\mathrm{prod}}|}.
\end{equation*}

The parameter $\tau$ determines the magnitude of $\btau$. In a previous zebrafish modeling work,
we determined $\tau$ heuristically by comparing simulated CSF velocity fields with in vivo
flow data~\cite{Olstad2019CiliaryDevelopment, Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion}. The resulting
tangential traction was of similar magnitude as the one determined by
Siyahhan~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles} and a similar parameter used
in modeling cilia-driven central canal flow in zebrafish
embryos~\cite{Thouvenin2020OriginCanal}. We therefore use the value determined by
Siyahhan~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Siyahhan2014FlowVentricles} to be able to compare
the impact of motile cilia with their results.

\subsection{Ventricular geometries and model scenarios}
We consider two geometries: the ventricular geometry of an embryonic zebrafish at two days
post-fertilization (2 dpf), and the brain ventricles of an adult (healthy) human. In embryonic zebrafish,
choroid plexus secretion begins at a later developmental stage than 2 dpf. Therefore, we only consider
cilia forces in this flow model, and the domain is closed.
For the human brain, we impose both deformation, choroid plexus
secretion and cilia forces, and the lateral apertures and the spinal canal are open boundaries.
To assess the role of the different flow mechanisms, we investigate three model scenarios
for the human brain model: (i) all three flow mechanisms (deformation, secretion, cilia);
(ii) deformation and cilia, no secretion; (iii) deformation and secretion, no cilia.

\subsection{Numerical methods}
We consider an $\bm{H}(\mathrm{div}; \DD)$-conforming discretization of 
the governing equations of fluid motion (\Crefrange{eq:navier_stokes_eq,eq:continuity_eq,
eq:time_dependent_stokes_eq,eq:stokes_eq}) using
Brezzi-Douglas-Marini (BDM) elements for the velocity and discontinuous Galerkin (DG)
elements for the pressure. The scheme conserves mass exactly pointwise.
The velocity component normal to facets is continuous
for the BDM elements. Tangential continuity is achieved by using the stabilization
outlined in Hong~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Hong2016AEquations}. 
For the Navier-Stokes equations, the convective term
is linearized as
$(\uu^{n+1}\cdot\nabla)\uu^{n+1} \approx (\uu^n\cdot\nabla)\uu^{n+1}$
where $n$ denotes timestep. Convective fluxes both internal in the
mesh and on open boundaries are stabilized with
an upwinding technique~\cite{Schroeder2018Towards}.

The cilia tangential traction boundary condition is imposed weakly,
while the displacement velocity and choroid plexus flux boundary
conditions are imposed strongly. A detailed derivation
of the BDM-DG discretization for the time-dependent 
Stokes equations can be found in
Herlyng~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Herlyng2025AdvectionDiffusion}.

In time, the fluid equations are discretized with the first-order, implicit
backward Euler scheme using a timestep $\Delta t = 0.001$ s.
Since we use an implicit time discretization,
an initial condition is only needed
for the velocity field, and not the pressure.
When solving the Stokes equations, the fluid is 
initially at rest: $\uu_0(\xx)=\uu(\xx, 0)=\bm{0}$.
For the Navier-Stokes equations, we solve the Stokes equations
(with $\uu_0(\xx)=\bm{0}$) and use the solution
$\uu_{\mathrm{Stk}}(\xx)$ as initial condition;
$\uu_0(\xx)=\uu_{\mathrm{Stk}}(\xx)$. 
The fluid equations are solved for five cardiac cycles,
and the velocity fields of the last cycle are used
to calculate FTLE fields.

The linear elasticity equations (\Cref{eq:strong_linear_elasticity_1})
are solved by a finite element
method using quadratic continuous Lagrange elements. In time, the
equations are discretized by a second-order central difference scheme.
We consider the system initially at rest: $\ww(\xx, t=0) = \bm{0}$.
The equations are solved for five cardiac cycles, and the last cycle
is used to calculate $\partial\ww/\partial t$ which is subsequently
used as a boundary condition in the fluid equations.

Convergence properties of the fluid discretization scheme were assessed with two
model problems. To assess spatial convergence, we solved a channel flow
problem, observing linear convergence in both the velocity error measured
in the $\bm{H}^1$ semi-norm and the pressure error measured in the $L^2$ norm.
Temporal convergence was assessed
with a Taylor vortex problem~\cite{Karniadakis2005Verification},
where the first-order accuracy of the backward Euler scheme was
confirmed. The discretization of the linear elasticity equations was
verified using the method of manufactured solutions~\cite{Roache2001CodeSolutions},
with the expected convergence rates confirmed.
